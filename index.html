NPH Audit Guide

Simple explanations for each data field

A step-by-step guide for medical students

👤

Patient Demographics

Hospital Number

The unique ID for the patient in your hospital system (e.g., MRN123456)

Why: Links all audit data to one patient and allows follow-up

Age

Patient's age in years at time of shunt surgery

Why: NPH is more common in elderly patients; age affects outcomes

Gender

Male/Female/Other

Why: Check if there are gender differences in presentation or outcomes

Ethnicity

Patient's ethnic background

Why: Helps identify if NPH affects certain populations differently

Post code

First part of patient's postal code (for anonymity)

Why: Shows geographic distribution and travel distances to clinic

📋

Referral & Initial Assessment

Date of initial referral

When the GP or another doctor first referred the patient to neurology/neurosurgery

Why: Helps calculate how long patients wait for assessment

Date of first clinic

When the patient was first seen in outpatient clinic

Why: Measures waiting times - important for service quality

Referral pathway - Local vs Non local

Was patient referred from your local area or from another region?

Why: Shows if you're a regional center; affects service planning

Referral to first clinic interval (days)

Calculate: Date of first clinic - Date of referral

Why: Key performance indicator; are patients seen quickly enough?

Duration of symptoms before intervention

How long in months/years did the patient have symptoms before getting the shunt?

Why: Longer symptom duration may mean worse outcomes

🩺

Pre-operative Clinical Assessment

Classic NPH triad documentation

Which of the three classic symptoms were present? Tick: Gait disturbance / Cognitive impairment / Urinary incontinence

Why: NPH diagnosis requires these symptoms; helps predict who responds to shunt

Pre-shunt gait assessment score

Use standardized tool (e.g., Timed Up and Go test, walking speed). Record the score and which test was used

Why: Objective baseline to compare post-shunt improvement

Pre-shunt cognition assessment score

Record score from MMSE, MoCA, or other cognitive test used

Why: Measures baseline cognitive function to track improvement

Urinary symptoms severity - Pre-shunt

Describe: None / Urgency / Frequency / Incontinence (or use urinary symptom score if documented)

Why: Urinary symptoms often improve with shunting; need baseline

Comorbidities

List other medical conditions: hypertension, diabetes, heart disease, etc.

Why: Comorbidities affect surgical risk and recovery

Charlston comorbidity index

A score (0-37) that quantifies medical comorbidities. Calculate or find in notes

Why: Standardized way to measure how sick the patient is overall

Previous treatments or interventions

List any prior treatments: medications, physiotherapy, previous surgeries

Why: Shows what was tried before shunting; affects outcomes

Pre-operative Imaging & Investigations

MRI available on our system?

Yes/No - Can you see their MRI scan on your hospital PACS?

Why: Quality check - should review imaging, not just reports

Pre-shunt imaging findings

Record Evans index (>0.3 suggests hydrocephalus), DESH sign, callosal angle (<90° suggests NPH)

Why: These features help diagnose NPH and predict shunt response

iNPH radscale

Mark if score was <4 or ≥4

Why: Score ≥4 suggests likely NPH

Date of LP/LD assessment

When did they have lumbar puncture or lumbar drain?

Why: Tracks timing of diagnostic workup

LP vs LD vs both

What test was done? Single lumbar puncture / Lumbar drain / Both

Why: Different tests; LD may be more sensitive but more invasive

CSF opening pressure

Record pressure in cmH₂O (normal: 10-20)

Why: NPH should have normal pressure; very high suggests different diagnosis

Volume of CSF removed during tap test

How many mL of CSF was taken out? Usually 30-50mL

Why: More volume removed may better predict shunt response

Days of LD drainage

If they had a lumbar drain, how many days? Usually 2-5 days

Why: Longer drainage improves prediction but increases infection risk

Documentation of outcome of the LD assessment

Was improvement documented? Positive/Negative/Equivocal

Why: Positive tap test strongly predicts good shunt response

Dementia markers sent?

Were CSF tests done for Alzheimer's? (Beta-amyloid, tau protein)

Why: Rules out other causes of dementia that won't respond to shunt

MDT & Decision Making

Date of MDT discussion

When was the case discussed at multidisciplinary team meeting?

Why: Good practice requires team discussion before major surgery

MDT members present

Who attended? E.g., Neurosurgeon, Neurologist, Geriatrician, Radiologist

Why: Shows quality of team input; diverse expertise improves decisions

MDT recommendations

What did the team suggest? Proceed with shunt / Further tests / Conservative

Why: Records clinical reasoning and shared decision making

MDT decision-making process - outcome

Final decision: Shunt / No shunt / Delayed decision

Why: Captures final consensus; important for governance

Diagnostic criteria used

Which guidelines? E.g., International iNPH guidelines, Japanese guidelines

Why: Ensures evidence-based practice; allows comparison with other centers

Prognostic score/likelihood of response

Was a prediction score calculated? Record percentage chance of improvement

Why: Helps set realistic expectations and inform consent

Reason for shunt vs no shunt decision

Brief explanation: "Positive tap test and classic triad" or "Too high surgical risk"

Why: Audit of clinical reasoning; learning from decisions

Clinic prior to shunt placement?

Was patient seen in clinic after tap test but before surgery?

Why: Shows pathway - some go straight to surgery, others reviewed first

⚕️

Operative Details

Date of admission

When patient came into hospital for surgery

Why: Helps calculate length of stay

Date of shunt placement

Actual surgery date - check operation note

Why: Key date for calculating all follow-up intervals

Type of valve used

Brand/model. E.g., Codman Hakim, Medtronic Strata, proGAV

Why: Different valves have different complication rates

Initial valve settings

For adjustable valves: initial pressure setting? E.g., "Level 1.5"

Why: Baseline for adjustments; different settings may affect outcomes

Lateralisation

Which side? Right/Left

Why: Surgeon preference; useful if comparing outcomes by side

Distal catheter location

Where does CSF drain? Peritoneal (abdomen) / Atrial (heart) / Pleural (chest)

Why: Peritoneal is commonest; others used if abdomen not suitable

Surgeon grade/experience

Who did it? Consultant / Registrar / Both (supervised)

Why: Training audit; may affect complication rates

Use of neuronavigation

Was computer guidance used? Yes/No

Why: Helps accurate catheter placement; may reduce revisions

Intraoperative complications

Any problems during surgery? None / Bleeding / Difficult placement / Other

Why: Important safety data; affects post-op management

🏥

Post-operative Care

Post-operative complications

Any problems? None / Infection / Subdural haematoma / Seizure / Confusion

Why: Critical safety measure; identifies high-risk patients

Discharge date

When patient went home

Why: Calculates length of stay

Length of hospital stay

Calculate: Discharge date - Admission date (in days)

Why: Efficiency measure; longer stays may indicate complications

Section 8: Follow-up & Outcomes

Follow-up duration

How many months from surgery to last clinic appointment?

Why: Shows how long patients are monitored; longer follow-up gives better outcome data

Number of clinics post shunt

Count outpatient appointments after surgery

Why: Resource use; complicated cases need more visits

Time of first clinic letter post shunt

How many weeks/months after surgery was first follow-up?

Why: Standard should be 6-12 weeks; quality check for timely review

Number of follow-up visits

Total number of times seen (clinic + emergency + other)

Why: Overall healthcare utilization

Number of ward attender reviews

How many times did patient come to day unit/ward for valve adjustments?

Why: Measures need for shunt optimization

Time to symptom improvement (weeks/months)

When did patient/family first notice improvement? Record in weeks or months

Why: Some improve quickly (days), others slowly (months); helps predict success

Which symptom improved most (gait/cognitive/urinary)

Gait / Cognition / Urinary / Equal / None

Why: Gait usually improves most; helps predict who benefits from shunting

Functional outcomes (symptom improvement, QoL)

Overall: Significant improvement / Mild improvement / No change / Worse

Why: Main outcome measure; was surgery worth it?

Patient-reported outcome measures used

Which questionnaires? E.g., NPH scale, SF-36, modified Rankin Scale

Why: Standardized tools give objective outcome data for comparison

Post-shunt gait assessment

Repeat same test as pre-op. Record score at best follow-up

Why: Compare to baseline; shows objective improvement in walking

Post-shunt cognitive assessment

Repeat cognitive test. Record best score

Why: Cognition may improve slower than gait; need objective measure

Post-shunt urinary assessment

Urinary symptoms at follow-up: Resolved / Improved / Same / Worse

Why: Complete picture of NPH triad improvement

Mobility status - aids post shunt

What help needed now? None / Stick / Walker / Wheelchair

Why: Practical measure of independence; compare to pre-op mobility

Caregiver burden assessment

If documented: did caregiver burden reduce? Yes/No/Not assessed

Why: Patient improvement should reduce family stress and caring responsibilities

🖼️

Section 9: Imaging Follow-up

Number of radiological scans post shunt

Count CT/MRI scans done after surgery (routine + emergency)

Why: Resource use; more scans may indicate problems or complications

First scan post shunt after discharge - number of weeks

How soon after going home was first scan? (Skip if done before discharge)

Why: Some centers do routine 6-week scans; identifies early complications

Post-shunt imaging results

Summary: Ventricles smaller / Same / Larger / Subdural collection / Other findings

Why: Smaller ventricles confirm shunt working; larger may mean blockage; subdurals suggest overdrainage

Section 10: Complications & Interventions

Shunt malfunction

Did shunt stop working? Yes/No (if yes, note when it happened)

Why: Common complication (10-40% of patients); affects long-term success

Shunt infection

Any infection? Yes/No (if yes, note when and organism if known)

Why: Serious complication (5-15% of cases); usually needs shunt removal and antibiotics

Shunt revision reason (overdrainage/underdrainage/mechanical)

If revised, why? Overdrainage (subdural) / Underdrainage (blocked) / Mechanical failure / Infection

Why: Identifies which problems are most common; helps improve future practice and valve selection

Re-intervention rates

How many operations after initial shunt? Count all revisions/adjustments requiring surgery

Why: Multiple operations indicate complexity; affects cost and patient burden

Morbidity (e.g., subdural haematoma)

Specific complications: Subdural collection / Seizures / Stroke / Other

Why: Safety monitoring; subdurals are common with overdrainage and may need treatment

Shunt setting changes - clinic letters

For programmable valves: were settings adjusted? How many times? What levels?

Why: Shows need for fine-tuning; programmable valves avoid repeat surgery

Changes in management/additional interventions

Any other treatments needed? Rehab / New medications / Other procedures

Why: Complete picture of patient journey and overall resource use

📝

Section 11: Final Outcome

RIP date

If patient died during follow-up period, record the date. Leave blank if patient is alive

Why: Mortality data; helps calculate survival rates; death may be related to surgery, shunt complications, or unrelated causes

💡 Top Tips for Data Collection

1.

Start with the operation note - it has the hospital number, dates, valve type, and surgical details

2.

Check clinic letters chronologically - first letter has baseline assessments, latest has outcomes

3.

Look for imaging reports in radiology system - don't just rely on written descriptions in letters

4.

If data is missing, write "Not documented" - this itself is useful audit information about documentation quality!

5.

Calculate dates carefully - use a date calculator online to avoid arithmetic errors

6.

Ask seniors if unclear - better to ask than guess and get data wrong

7.

Be consistent - if you use "Timed Up and Go" for one patient, use it for all patients

8.

Double-check hospital numbers - wrong patient = all your data becomes useless

📚 Common Abbreviations You'll See

LP: Lumbar Puncture (spinal tap)
LD: Lumbar Drain
CSF: Cerebrospinal Fluid
MDT: Multidisciplinary Team
DESH: Disproportionately Enlarged Subarachnoid Spaces
VP shunt: Ventriculoperitoneal shunt (to abdomen)
MMSE: Mini-Mental State Examination
MoCA: Montreal Cognitive Assessment
QoL: Quality of Life
PACS: Picture Archiving System (where scans are stored)
